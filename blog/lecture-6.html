Sufficient Statistics and the Exponential Family

Probability distributions that belong to an exponential family have natural conjugate prior distributions. The form is:

$$p(y_i \vert \theta) = f(y_i)g(\theta) e^{\phi(\theta)^{T} u(y_i)}.$$

Thus the likelihood corresponding to n i.i.d. points is:

$$ p(y \vert \theta) = \left(\prod_{i=1}^{n} f(y_i) \right) g(\theta)^n \,\, \exp\left(\phi(\theta)\sum_{i=1}^{n} u(y_i)\right)$$

For all n and y, this has a fixed form as a functio of $\theta$:

$$ p(y \vert \theta) = g(\theta)^n \, {e^{\phi (\theta)}}^T t(y)$$

where $t(y) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} u(y_i)$ is said to be a sufficient statistic for $\theta$ , because the likelihood for theta depends on y only through $t(y)$.

It has been shown that in general, the exponential families are the only classes of distributions that have natural conjugate prior distributions, since, apart from some special cases, they are the only distributions having a fixed number of sufficient statisticsfor all $n$.

This family includes exponential, poisson, gamma, beta, pareto and even the binomial (through $logit(\theta)$).

An example

In 1990s data gathered on the educational attainment and number of children of 155 women who were 40 years of age at the time of the survey. In this example we are interested in the birth rate of women with college degrees compared to those without. Let $Y_{1,1}, \ldots, Y_{n_1,1}$ denote the number of children for the $n_1$ women without college degrees and $Y_{1,2}, \ldots, Y_{n_2,2}$ be the data for $n_2$ women with college degrees.

Lets assume that the number of children of a women in any one of these classes can me modelled as coming from ONE birth rate (we dont know anything about their individual situations so we treat each woman as interchangeable with another within the same class: i.e. iid variables): $$Y_{n_i,1|2} \sim Poisson(\theta_{1|2}). $$

Then, the likelihood for the first population is:

$$ p(Y_{1,1}, \ldots, Y_{n_1,1} | \theta_1) = \prod_{i=1}^{n_1} p(Y_{i,1}|\theta_1) = \prod_{i=1}^{n_1} \frac{1}{Y_{i,1} !} \theta_1^{Y_{i,1}} e^{-\theta_1} = c(Y_{1,1}, \ldots, Y_{n_1,1}) \,\, (n_{1}\theta_{1})^{\sum Y_{i,1}} e^{-n_1 \theta_1} \sim Poisson(n_1 \theta_1) $$

and similarly

$$ Y_{1,2}, \ldots, Y_{n_1,2} | \theta_2 \sim Poisson(n_2\theta_2) $$

The distributions are still poisson

The posterior ia a simple product of two sub-posteriors:

$$p(\theta_1| Y_{1,1}, \ldots, Y_{n_1,1} )* p(\theta_2| Y_{1,2}, \ldots, Y_{n_2,2} ) ,$$ which, given independent priors on

$\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$, is:

$$c_1(n_1, y_1, \ldots, y_{n_1}) \,\, (n_{1}\theta_{1})^{\sum Y_{i,1}} e^{-n_1 \theta_1}\, p(\theta_1) \times c_2(n_2, y_1, \ldots, y_{n_2}) \,\, (n_{2}\theta_{2}^{\sum Y_{i,2}} e^{-n_2 \theta_2} \, p(\theta_2) $$

The quantity $\sum Y_i$ contains all the information about $\theta$ that is available in the data and we say that $\sum Y_i$ is sufficient statistics. Indeed all you need is the total number of kids in each class as far as making any inferences about $theta$ are concerned.

For our example say that $n_1 =111$, $\sum_i^{n_1} Y_{i,1} =217$ and $n_2=44$, $\sum_i^{n_2} Y_{i,2} =66$.

Lets now choose priors. A class of prior densities is conjugate for a sampling mode $p(y_1, \ldots, y_n| \theta)$ if the posterior is also in the class. For the Poisson sampling model, our posterior distribution for $\theta$ has the following form

$$ p( \theta | Y_1, \ldots, Y_n) \propto p(\theta) p(Y_1, \ldots, y_n| \theta) = p(\theta) \theta^{\sum Y_i} e^{-n \theta} $$

So that means our conjugate class has to include terms like $\theta^{c_1} e^{-c_2 \theta}$. This is a known family known as Gamma distributions.

$$p(\theta) = \rm{Gamma}(\theta, a,1/b) = \frac{b^a}{\Gamma(a)} \theta^{a-1} e^{-b \theta} $$

If $p(\theta) = \rm{Gamma}(\theta, a,1/b)$ and $ p(Y_1 \ldots, Y_n | \theta) \sim \rm{Poisson}(\theta) $ then

$$ p( \theta | Y_1, \ldots, Y_n) \sim \rm{Gamma}(\theta, a+\sum Y_i,1/( b+n)) $$

Back to our example. We choose prior $p(\theta_{1,2}) \sim \rm{Gamma}(\theta_{1,2}, a=2, b=1) $ therefore

$$ p(\theta_1|n_1 = 111, \sum_i^{n_1} Y_{i,1}=217 ) \sim \rm{Gamma}(\theta_1, 2+217, 1/( 1+111)) = \rm{Gamma}(\theta_1, 219, 1/112) $$

$$ p(\theta_2|n_2 = 44, \sum_i^{n_2} Y_{i,2}=66 ) \sim \rm{Gamma}(\theta_2, 2+66, 1/(1+44)) = \rm{Gamma}(\theta_2, 68,1/45) $$

The mean and variance of gamma distributions are known

$$ E[\theta] = a/b, var[\theta] = a/b^2 $$

But how about the Monte Carlo method? Firstly we can choose any prior we feel like without the need to choose a conjugate prior and we shall see in the following example. Moreover, we would like to know

$$ P( \theta_1 > \theta_2 \vert \sum_i^{n_1} Y_{i,1} =217 , \sum_i^{n_2} Y_{i,2} =66) = \int_{0}^{\infty} \int_{\theta_1}^{\infty} p(\theta_1, \theta_2 \vert y_{1,1}, \ldots , y_{2,n_2}) d\theta_1 \, d\theta_2 $$

We can calculate this integral in multiple methods but the easiest and most general method is our beloved Monte Carlo.

Back to the Gaussian: joint distribution of mean and variance

In the last lecture we did the case of the gaussian with known $\sigma^2$ but unknown mean, with a gaussian prior. But how about if we did not know $\sigma^2$?

This is conceptually the same as the one-parameter case. First recall the Kolmogorov definition

$$p(\theta, \sigma^2) = p(\theta | \sigma^2) \; p(\sigma^2) $$

For $\sigma^2$ we need a prior that covers (0,$\infty$). One option is the gamma family. Unfortunately this is not conjugate for the normal. However it turns out to be conjugate for pdf of $1/\sigma^2$. This is called the inverse-gamma distribution:

$$ p(\sigma^2) \sim \rm{inversegamma}(a,b) $$

Under this parametrization $ E[\sigma^2] = \frac{b}{a-1} $.

Let our prior have this form

$$ p(\sigma^2) \sim \rm{inversegamma}( \frac{ \nu}{2}, \frac{\nu \sigma_0^2}{2}) $$

and we know the conditional prior of $\theta$

$$ p( \theta | \sigma^2) \sim N (\mu, \sigma^2/\kappa) $$

This joint prior can be decomposed as $p(\theta, \sigma^2)=p(\theta| \sigma^2) p(\sigma^2) $, and therefore the posterior can be similarly decomposed:

$$ p(\theta, \sigma^2 | y_1,\ldots,y_n) = p( \theta | \sigma^2) \; p(\sigma^2 ) p(y_1\ldots,y_n \vert \sigma^2, \theta)$$

The conditional distribution of $\theta$ given the data and $\sigma^2$ can be obtained using the results of the previous section. The posterior distribution of $\sigma^2$ can be obtained by performing an integration over known values of $\theta$:

$$ p(\sigma^2|y_1, \ldots, y_n) \sim p(\sigma^2) p(y_1, \ldots, y_n | \sigma^2) = p(\sigma^2) \int p(y_1, \ldots, y_n | \theta, \sigma^2 ) \, p(\theta | \sigma^2) d\theta $$

One can integrate this (left as an exercise) but the result is:

$$ p(1/\sigma^2| y_1, \ldots, y_n) \sim \rm{gamma}(\nu_{n} /2, \nu_{n} \sigma^2_{n}/2) $$ where

$$ \nu_n = \nu+n $$

$$ \sigma_n^2 = \frac{1}{\nu_n} \left[ \nu \sigma_0^2 +(n-1) \, s^2 + \frac{ \kappa n }{\kappa+ n}(\bar{y} - \mu)^2 \right] $$

and $s^2$ is the sample variance.

Example: Moth wing example (joint distribution):

Studies of other populations suggest that the true mean and standard deviation of our population under study should not be too far the sample mean. We use here $\mu=19$ and $\sigma_0^2 = 2$. This population may be different from others so we choose $\kappa = \nu=1$. The sample mean $\bar{y} = 18.14$ and sample variance $s^2=1.9928$. We can compute the posterior means

$$ \mu_p = \frac{\kappa}{\kappa+n} \mu + \frac{n}{\kappa+n} \bar{y} = 18.2260 $$

$$ \sigma_p^2 = \frac{1}{\nu_n} \left[ \nu \sigma_0^2 + (n-1) s^2 + \frac{\kappa \, n}{\kappa + n} (\bar{y} - \mu)^2 \right] = 1.8606 $$

So the posterior distribution is determined by the values $\mu_p= 18.2260, \kappa =1 , \sigma_p^2 = 1.8606, \nu=1 $. Let $\hat{\sigma}^2= 1/\sigma^2$ we can create a contour plot by computing

$$ N\left(\theta_k, \mu_p, 1/\sqrt{10 \hat{ \sigma}_l^2} \right) \times \rm{gamma}\left(\hat{\sigma}_l^2, 10/2, 10 \sigma_p^2/2 \right) $$

for each pair $\theta_k, \hat{\sigma}_l^2 $ on a grid.

Priors - not for my grandmother

Though conjugate priors are nice and we can build all these easy examples, it is very unsettling when the priors strongly inﬂuence the posterior distribution. What we want are uninformative priors.

Why not assign flat priors? Since we do not know where the actual value lies in the parameter space, we might as well consider all values with equal probability. This prior however is not transformation invariant. What does this mean? Suppose we have a random variable $X$ from Bernoulli($\theta$). We define the prior $p(\theta)=1$.

Uninformative location prior

Consider the case where we have a location parameter: a probability distribution over a variable X of density f (X − θ) where θ is a location parameter. A candidate for a prior would be $p(θ) \propto 1$. If θ lies in an interval, we can consider the uniform distribution as a prior estimate. If θ can take any value in R, the ﬂat prior is not a probability density because it does not integrate to a finite number. Such a prior is called an improper prior. It expresses our state of ignorance (hence the ﬂat prior).

Uninformative scaling prior

The point of Jeffreys Priors is to create priors that don't become informative when you transform the variables. Jeffreys Priors are defined as:

$$p_J(θ)\propto {\bf I}(θ)^{1/2}$$ $${\bf I}(θ)=−E\left[ \frac{d^{2} \log p(X|θ)}{dθ^2} \right]$$

I being the Fisher information.

Which I hate with passion, for this contradicts what I said earlier: "we should not be using observations to define priors". This is a long discussion and will be discussed at Lab3.

Weakly Informative Prior

A related idea is the weakly informative prior, which attempts to 'regularize' the posterior by keeping it within reasonable bounds, but which tries not to affect the data as much as possible. Thus for example, ome night restrict a uniform prior to a specific range., or broaden a highly informative gaussian prior to diffuse the information.

Priors loose their powers as N increases

In the example of the linear regression I demonstrated (not very well) the effect of the prior as the number of observations (data) increases/decreases. This is kind of obvious. After all, prior is our previous belief, and eventually enough new information will overwelmed our previous belief. The derease of the influence of the prior by enough data is also good news: if our prior is significantly wrong, then the data will correct that eventually we will presented with the correct posterior.

Lets demonstrate this with an example. We look at the case where the likelihood is a Bernoulli and we choose two Beta priors, one a flat prior and the other biased towards 0 (Beta is the conjugate prior for the Bernoulli). We look at the parameter $\theta$ of the two posteriors. As the sample size increases, the posteriors converge.

The Beta is conjugate prior to Bernouli and the hypermarameters (parameters of the distribution function) are given

$$ Beta\left(a+\sum_i x_i ,b+n-\sum_i x_i\right)=Beta(a,b) \, \, \rm{Bernoulli(x|\theta)}$$
